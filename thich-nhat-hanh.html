Thich Nhat Hanh

Take a quick walk through the Zen Master's life

1950s

The beginning

In the 1950s, Thich Nhat Hanh was a young monk and teacher in Vietnam. He established the An Quang Buddhist Academy in Saigon, which aimed to provide a modern education that integrated Buddhist teachings with subjects such as literature, history, and social sciences. He also founded the Van Hanh Buddhist University, which aimed to provide higher education and training for Buddhist monks. Along with teaching, Thich Nhat Hanh also worked to promote peace and reconciliation during a time of great turmoil in Vietnam. He traveled around the country, giving talks and workshops on mindfulness, compassion, and nonviolence, and he also advocated for the peaceful resolution of the ongoing conflict. In the 1950s he also started to travel abroad, to France, Japan and India where he was able to present his vision of engaged Buddhism to a wider audience. He also began to publish articles and books, which helped to make his name known internationally.

1962

School of Youth and Social Service

The School of Youth and Social Services, also known as SYSS, is a program founded by Vietnamese Zen master and peace activist Thich Nhat Hanh. The program is designed to provide young people with the skills and knowledge necessary to become leaders in promoting peace and social change in their communities. Through a combination of mindfulness practice, community service, and social action, SYSS participants learn to address the root causes of suffering and work towards creating a more just and compassionate society. Additionally, the program aims to foster a deep sense of interconnection and compassion for all living beings, as well as the skills of deep listening, dialogue, and conflict resolution.

1966

Order of Inter Being

The Order of Interbeing, also known as the Order of Interdependent Origination, is a monastic order founded by Thich Nhat Hanh as an extension of the Plum Village Community of Engaged Buddhism. The Order is based on the principles of interdependence and interbeing, which state that all things in the universe are interconnected and interdependent, and that our actions and decisions have an impact on the world around us. Members of the Order take the Fourteen Mindfulness Trainings, which are a set of moral guidelines that emphasize the importance of non-violence, compassion, and understanding in all aspects of life. They also commit to living a monastic lifestyle, which includes living in a community, practicing mindfulness and meditation, and engaging in service and social action.

1967

Martin Luther King

In 1966, Vietnamese Zen master and peace activist Thich Nhat Hanh met with American civil rights leader Martin Luther King Jr. during Nhat Hanh's visit to the United States. The meeting was arranged by a mutual friend and took place at the home of civil rights leader James Lawson in Nashville, Tennessee. During the meeting, Nhat Hanh and King discussed the use of nonviolence as a means of achieving social change, and King expressed his support for Nhat Hanh's efforts to promote peace and reconciliation in Vietnam. King also wrote a public letter to the American Friends Service Committee, endorsing Nhat Hanh's work, in which he wrote: "I have been particularly impressed by his ideas for the rehabilitation of Vietnam. I feel that we cannot begin to withdraw from that struggle without at the same time undertaking to engage in the struggle for the improvement of the human condition here and now."

1982

Established Plum Village

Plum Village is a Buddhist community founded by Thich Nhat Hanh in 1982, located in the Dordogne region of France. It is the main practice center for the Plum Village Community of Engaged Buddhism, and is also home to the European Institute of Applied Buddhism. The community is based on the principles of mindfulness, compassion and interbeing, and offers a variety of programs and retreats for both lay people and monastics. Visitors can participate in mindfulness practice, including sitting and walking meditation, as well as engage in daily life activities such as gardening, cooking, and working together in silence. The community also offers retreats for families, young adults, and people of color, as well as programs for socially engaged Buddhism, such as training for activists and peacemakers.

2014

Tragic Stroke

Thich Nhat Hanh, a Vietnamese Zen Master and peace activist, suffered a severe stroke in November 2014. The stroke left him unable to speak or move and he was placed in a medically induced coma for several weeks. He was later transferred to a rehabilitation center in France, where he received extensive physical therapy and rehabilitation. Despite the severity of the stroke, Thich Nhat Hanh made a remarkable recovery and was able to return to Plum Village, his Buddhist community in France. He communicated through a system of hand signals and gestures, and was able to resume teaching and writing. Thich Nhat Hanh's stroke was a reminder of the fragility of human life and the importance of taking care of our physical and mental health.

2022

Passing

Thich Nhat Hanh, a Vietnamese Zen master, peace activist, and author, passed away on January 2022. His death was mourned by many people around the world, including spiritual leaders, politicians, and regular people who were touched by his teachings. Thich Nhat Hanh's legacy continues to live on through the Plum Village Community of Engaged Buddhism, which he founded, and through the many students and practitioners who continue to follow his teachings. His death marked the passing of one of the most well-known and respected Zen Masters of our time, who had a significant impact on Buddhism, mindfulness and peace.
